New Title IX Proposal

Secretary of Education, Betsy DeVos, and the Department of Education have recently suggested changes to the way in which public universities are required to handle Title IX procedures, which is the guiding law that ensures equality between sexes at public institutions. These changes will impact all who attend public universities, such as UMD. Over a 60 day period, ending January 30th, you have the opportunity to submit a “public comment,” sharing with Secretary DeVos & the Department of Education your thoughts on the proposed changes. See below to learn about how these changes will impact your life at UMD, how to submit a public comment, and more on the history of Title IX.

Title IX at UMD

UMD has a long history with Title IX. Bernice "Bunny" Sandler, UMD alumna, is credited as the "God Mother" of Title IX. In 1969, Bunny graduated with her Ed.D. from UMD, and quickly experienced predujice in hiring processes post-graduation. Sparked by her experience, she aided Representative Patsy Mink and Senator Birch Bayh to birth the greatest change to modern collegiate equality, Title IX.

How to Submit a Public Comment

Submitting a public comment is easy and only takes a few minutes! Simply follow this link to submit online through regulations.gov. Regulations.gov is the federal government's platform to post Federal regulations. Through the website, one can read about changes in federal regulations and submit their thoughts, or “public comments,” on proposed changes.

To help you get started on your public comment, we have created this template which you may choose to follow, or you may write a comment entirely on your own. Additionally, if you scroll down this website you will find explanations of the four main proposed changes to Title IX, as well as the history of Title IX from its inception to present day.

How the Changes Will Affect UMD Students

Off-Campus Assaults Unprotected

Under the proposed changes, public institutions would not be required to investigate off-campus incidents. As recent as October 2018, 59.6% of UMD students lived off-campus, leaving over half of all UMD students unprotected outside of their academic lives. Areas considered “off-campus” at UMD include off-campus apartment complexes, such as Terrapin Row, The Varsity, & University View, local bars, restaurants, and several of the fraternity and sorority houses.

Accused Can Cross Examine Accusers

At UMD, we currently offer both complainants and respondents the opportunity to ask questions of the other party. These questions go through a moderator to ensure no inappropriate questions are asked. Under new guidance, advisors could ask unmodified questions which could be traumatic for survivors.

Definition of Sexual Harassment Narrowed

Public institutions currently define sexual harassment in accordance with the Obama-era guidelines. The proposed, narrowed definition of harassment requires that a student experience harassment repeatedly before they can seek justice.

Desirable Time Period Removed

At UMD, we currently seek to complete investigations within 60 business days in order to stay compliant under the Title IX law. The suggested changes specify no time period for the investigations to be completed within, which may make it more difficult to hold the process accountable when investigations are unreasonably long. Under the current regulations, UMD does not always complete investigations within the 60 days.

History of Title IX

  • Bernice Sandler Begins Research

    Bernice "Bunny" Sandler graduates from the University of Maryland with her Ed.D. and begins experiencing discrimination in her job hunt, prompting her to begin intensive research on U.S. civil rights. This research later would be pivotal in creating Title IX.

    May 1969

  • Critical Hearings Are Held

    Representative Edith Green and Patsy Mink connect with Bunny and use her materials to hold the first hearings on sex discrimination in higher education.

    June 1970

  • Title IX Reaches Senate Floor

    Senator Birch Bayh introduces Title IX as an amendment to the Higher Education Act of 1965, which was at the time on the Senate floor for re-authorization.

    Feb 1972

  • Title IX Becomes Law

    President Richard Nixon signs Title IX into public law.

    June 1972

  • Obama-Era Title IX Guidance

    The Obama Administration announces new guidelines for public institutions to follow, including increased protection for survivors and recommending a maximum of 60 day adjudications.

    April 2011

  • Betsy DeVos Introduces Proposed Changes

    Betsy DeVos and the Department of Education announce the proposed changes to how public institutions carry out the goal of Title IX. Much of the recommendations undo the Obama-era recommendations.

    Oct 2018
